## Notes on Linear PDEs and Laplace’s Equation
_Based on Evans, Partial Differential Equations (Sections 2.1 and 2.2)_

### 1. The Transport Equation
The transport equation is the most fundamental first-order PDE. It describes the motion of a scalar field $u$ moving with a constant velocity vector $b \in \mathbb{R}^n$.

#### 1.1 The Homogeneous Problem
Consider the equation:
$$u_t + b \cdot Du = 0 \quad \text{in } \mathbb{R}^n \times (0, \infty)$$

**Conceptual Insight:**
This equation states that the directional derivative of $u$ in the space-time direction $(b, 1)$ is zero. This means $u$ is constant along specific lines called characteristics. One can visualize this by thinking that any plane cutting the space with perpendicular vector $(b,1)$ has in it the "same" graph.

**The Method of Characteristics:**
To find $u(x, t)$, we define a path $z(s) := u(x + sb, t + s)$. Applying the chain rule:
$$\dot{z}(s) = Du(x + sb, t + s) \cdot b + u_t(x + sb, t + s)$$
From our PDE, this sum is $0$. Therefore, $z(s)$ is constant. By tracing the line back to the initial time $t=0$, we find:
$$u(x, t) = g(x - tb)$$

### 2. Laplace’s Equation
Laplace’s equation $\Delta u = 0$ is the prototype for elliptic PDEs, describing physical systems in equilibrium.

#### 2.1 Physical Interpretation: Flux
If $\mathbf{F}$ represents a flux density, equilibrium implies that the net flux through the boundary of any subregion $V$ is zero:
$$\int_{\partial V} \mathbf{F} \cdot \nu \, dS = 0 \implies \text{div } \mathbf{F} = 0$$
Using Fourier’s Law ($\mathbf{F} = -a Du$), we derive:
$$\text{div}(-a Du) = 0 \implies \Delta u = 0$$

#### 2.2 The Fundamental Solution
By looking for radial solutions ($u(x) = v(|x|)$), we derive the Fundamental Solution $\Phi(x)$:
$$\Phi(x) := \begin{cases} -\frac{1}{2\pi} \log|x| & (n=2) \\ 
\frac{1}{n(n-2)\alpha(n)} \frac{1}{|x|^{n-2}} & (n \geq 3) 
\end{cases}$$

### 3. Mean-Value Formulas
The Mean-Value Property (MVP) states that the value at a point is exactly the average of its neighbors.
**Theorem:** If $u \in C^2(U)$ is harmonic, then for any ball $B(x, r) \subset U$:
$$u(x) = \dashint_{\partial B(x, r)} u \, dS = \dashint_{B(x, r)} u \, dy$$



---

### 4. The Strong Maximum Principle
Suppose $u$ is harmonic in a connected open set $U$.
* **Weak Version:** The maximum of $u$ must be on the boundary $\partial U$.
* **Strong Version:** If $u$ attains its maximum at any interior point, then $u$ must be constant everywhere in $U$.

### Exercises

#### (1) Transport Equation with Decay
**Given the problem:**
$$\begin{cases} u_t + b \cdot Du + cu = 0 & (\mathbb{R}^n \times \mathbb{R}^+) \\ u = g & (\mathbb{R}^n \times \{0\}) \end{cases}$$

**Solution:**
We define $x(s)$ and $t(s)$ as functions of $s$:
1. $\frac{dt}{ds} = 1 \implies t(s) = s$
2. $\frac{dx}{ds} = b \implies x(s) = x_0 + sb$

Put $z(s) = u(x(s), t(s))$. Then:

$$\frac{dz}{ds}(s) = u_t \cdot \frac{dt}{ds} + Du \cdot \frac{dx}{ds} = u_t + b \cdot Du$$

From the PDE, $u_t + b \cdot Du = -cu$. We get the ODE:

$$\frac{dz}{ds} = -c \cdot z(s) \implies z(s) = z_0 \cdot e^{-cs}$$

Since $z_0 = g(x_0)$ and $x_0 = x - tb$, setting $s=t$ gives:

**$$u(x, t) = g(x - tb)e^{-ct}$$**

#### (2) Rotation Invariance of Laplace's Equation
If $\Delta u = 0$, show that $\Delta v = 0$ where $v(x) := u(Ox)$ for an orthogonal matrix $O$.

**Solution:**
Let $y = Ox$, so $y_j = \sum_{k=1}^n O_{jk} x_k$. By the chain rule:

$$\frac{\partial v}{\partial x_i} = \sum_{j=1}^n O_{ji} \frac{\partial u}{\partial y_j}$$

The second derivative is:

$$\frac{\partial^2 v}{\partial x_i^2} = \sum_{j=1}^n O_{ji} \left( \sum_{k=1}^n O_{ki} \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial y_j \partial y_k} \right)$$

Summing over $i$:

$$\Delta v = \sum_{j,k=1}^n \left( \sum_{i=1}^n O_{ji} O_{ki} \right) \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial y_j \partial y_k}$$

Since $O$ is orthogonal, $\sum O_{ji} O_{ki} = \delta_{jk}$. Thus:

**$$\Delta v = \sum_{j=1}^n \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial y_j^2} = 0$$**

#### (3) Poisson's Equation Fundamental Solution
**Claim:** $u(0) = \dashint_{\partial B(0,r)} g \, dS + \frac{1}{n(n-2)\alpha(n)} \int_{B(0,r)} \left( \frac{1}{|x|^{n-2}} - \frac{1}{r^{n-2}} \right) f \, dx$

**Solution:**
Define $\phi(r) = \dashint_{\partial B(0,r)} u \, dS$. From Green's formulas:

$$\phi'(r) = \frac{r}{n} \dashint_{B(0,r)} \Delta u \, dy = -\frac{1}{n \alpha(n) r^{n-1}} \int_{B(0,r)} f \, dy$$

Using the FTC, $\phi(r) - \phi(0) = \int_0^r \phi'(s) \, ds$. Since $\phi(0) = u(0)$:

$$u(0) = \dashint_{\partial B(0,r)} g \, dS + \frac{1}{n \alpha(n)} \int_{B(0,r)} f(y) \left( \int_{|y|}^r \frac{1}{s^{n-1}} \, ds \right) dy$$

Integrating leads to the formula.

#### (4) Weak Maximum Principle
**Claim:** $\max_{\bar{U}} u = \max_{\partial U} u$ for $\Delta u = 0$.

**Solution:**
Define $u_\epsilon := u + \epsilon |x|^2$. Then $\Delta u_\epsilon = 2n\epsilon > 0$.

If $u_\epsilon$ had an interior maximum, $\Delta u_\epsilon \leq 0$, which is a contradiction.

Thus, $\max_{\bar{U}} u_\epsilon = \max_{\partial U} u_\epsilon$.

Taking $\epsilon \to 0$:

**$$\max_{\bar{U}} u = \max_{\partial U} u$$**

#### (5) Subharmonic Functions
**Definition:** $v \in C^2(U)$ is subharmonic if $-\Delta v \leq 0$.

**a) Claim:** $v(x) \leq \dashint_{B(x,r)} v \, dy$

**Proof:** $\phi'(r) = \frac{r}{n} \dashint_{B(x,r)} \Delta v \, dy \geq 0$. Thus $\phi(r)$ is non-decreasing. As $r \to 0$, $\phi(r) \to v(x)$, so $v(x) \leq \phi(r)$.

**b)** Directly follows the proof in Exercise 4.

**c) Claim:** If $\Delta u = 0$ and $\phi$ is convex, $v := \phi(u)$ is subharmonic.

**Proof:** $\Delta v = \phi'' (u) \mid Du\mid^2 + \phi'(u) \Delta u$. Since $\Delta u = 0$ and $\phi'' \geq 0$, $\Delta v \geq 0$.

**d) Claim:** If $\Delta u = 0$, then $v := \mid Du\mid ^2$ is subharmonic.

**Proof:** $\Delta v = 2 \sum (u_{x_i x_j}^2 + u_{x_i} u_{x_i x_j x_j})$. Since $\Delta u = 0$, we have:
**$$\Delta v = 2 |D^2 u|^2 \geq 0$$**
