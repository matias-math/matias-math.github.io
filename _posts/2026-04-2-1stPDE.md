### 1. The Transport Equation

The transport equation is the most fundamental first-order PDE. It describes the motion of a scalar field $u$ moving with a constant velocity vector $b \in \mathbb{R}^n$.

#### 1.1 The Homogeneous Problem

Consider the equation:

$$u_t + b \cdot Du = 0 \quad \text{in } \mathbb{R}^n \times (0, \infty)$$

**Conceptual Insight:**

This equation states that the directional derivative of $u$ in the space-time direction $(b, 1)$ is zero. This means $u$ is constant along specific lines called **characteristics**.

**The Method of Characteristics:**

To find $u(x, t)$, we define a path $z(s) := u(x + sb, t + s)$. Applying the chain rule:

$$\dot{z}(s) = Du(x + sb, t + s) \cdot b + u_t(x + sb, t + s)$$

From our PDE, this sum is $0$. Therefore, $z(s)$ is constant. By tracing the line back to the initial time $t=0$, we find:

$$u(x, t) = g(x - tb)$$

### 2. Laplace's Equation

Laplace’s equation $\Delta u = 0$ is the prototype for elliptic PDEs, describing physical systems in equilibrium (steady-state temperature, electrostatic potential).

#### 2.1 Physical Interpretation: Flux

If $\mathbf{F}$ represents a flux density (like heat flow), equilibrium implies that the net flux through the boundary of any subregion $V$ is zero:

$$\int_{\partial V} \mathbf{F} \cdot \nu \, dS = 0 \implies \text{div } \mathbf{F} = 0$$

Using **Fick’s Law** or **Fourier's Law** ($\mathbf{F} = -a Du$), we derive:

$$\text{div}(-a Du) = 0 \implies \Delta u = 0$$

#### 2.2 The Fundamental Solution

By looking for **radial solutions** ($u(x) = v(|x|)$), we derive the "building block" of potential theory, the **Fundamental Solution** $\Phi(x)$:

$$\Phi(x) := \begin{cases} -\frac{1}{2\pi} \log|x| & (n=2) \\ \frac{1}{n(n-2)\alpha(n)} \frac{1}{|x|^{n-2}} & (n \geq 3) \end{cases}$$

### 3. Mean-Value Formulas

The Mean-Value Property (MVP) is the "DNA" of harmonic functions. It states that the value at a point is exactly the average of its neighbors.

**Theorem:** If $u \in C^2(U)$ is harmonic, then for any ball $B(x, r) \subset U$:

$$u(x) = \dashint_{\partial B(x, r)} u \, dS = \dashint_{B(x, r)} u \, dy$$

**Key Proof Logic:**

1. Define $\phi(r)$ as the average of $u$ over the sphere $\partial B(x, r)$.
    
2. Show that $\phi'(r) = \frac{r}{n} \dashint_{B(x, r)} \Delta u \, dy$ using **Green's Formulas**.
    
3. Since $\Delta u = 0$, $\phi'(r) = 0$, meaning the average doesn't change with $r$.
    
4. As $r \to 0$, the average must be the value $u(x)$.
    

---

### 4. The Strong Maximum Principle

Because of the Mean-Value Property, harmonic functions are extremely "rigid." They cannot have local bumps or dips in the interior of their domain.

**The Theorem:**

Suppose $u$ is harmonic in a connected open set $U$.

- **Weak Version:** The maximum of $u$ must be on the boundary $\partial U$.
    
- **Strong Version:** If $u$ attains its maximum at any **interior point**, then $u$ must be constant everywhere in $U$.
    

**Topological Logic:**

If $u(x_0) = M$ (a maximum), then the MVP says $M$ is the average of $u$ in a small ball. This only works if $u \equiv M$ in that ball. By "chaining" these balls together (connectivity), the constant value $M$ spreads across the entire domain.

## Exercises

### 1. The Transport Equation: A Total Derivative View
As annotated in the notes, the homogeneous transport equation $u_t + b \cdot Du = 0$ can be viewed through the lens of a **Total Derivative**.

#### 1.1 Construction via ODE
If we define space $x$ and time $t$ as functions of a parameter $s$:
* Set $\frac{dt}{ds} = 1 \implies t(s) = s$
* Set $\frac{dx}{ds} = b \implies x(s) = x_0 + sb$

Now, define the path function $z(s) = u(x(s), t(s))$. By the multivariable chain rule:
$$\frac{dz}{ds}(s) = u_t \cdot \frac{dt}{ds} + Du \cdot \frac{dx}{ds} = u_t + b \cdot Du$$

Since the original PDE tells us $u_t + b \cdot Du = 0$, we have $\frac{dz}{ds} = 0$. Thus, $u$ is constant along the line $(x_0 + sb, s)$. Tracing back to $s=0$:
$$u(x, t) = g(x - tb)$$



#### 1.2 Adding a Linear Decay Term
If the equation includes a term $cu$, such that $u_t + b \cdot Du = -cu$, our ODE becomes:
$$\frac{dz}{ds} = -cz(s) \implies z(s) = z_0 e^{-cs}$$
Substituting back the initial condition $z_0 = g(x_0)$, we get the solution:
**$$u(x, t) = g(x - tb)e^{-ct}$$**

### 2. Harmonic Functions and the Mean Value Property (MVP)
A function $u$ is **harmonic** if $\Delta u = 0$. This equilibrium state grants the function unique averaging properties.

#### 2.1 The Mean Value Formulas
For any ball $B(x, r) \subset U$:
$$u(x) = \dashint_{\partial B(x, r)} u \, dS = \dashint_{B(x, r)} u \, dy$$

**The "Surface to Volume" Logic:**
As seen in the notes, we derive the volume average from the surface average by using polar coordinates:
$$\int_{B(x, r)} u(y) \, dy = \int_0^r \left( \int_{\partial B(x, s)} u \, dS \right) ds$$
Substituting $u(x) n \alpha(n) s^{n-1}$ for the inner integral and integrating with respect to $s$ yields the volume average formula.

### 3. Subharmonic Functions

#### 3.1 Definition
A function $v \in C^2(U)$ is **said subharmonic** if:
$$-\Delta v \leq 0 \quad (\text{or } \Delta v \geq 0 \text{ in } U)$$

#### 3.2 Key Property: The Integral Inequality
If $v$ is subharmonic, the value at the center is less than or equal to the average:
$$v(x) \leq \dashint_{B(x, r)} v \, dy$$
This is proven by showing that the function $\phi(r) = \dashint_{\partial B(x, r)} v \, dS$ is non-decreasing ($\phi'(r) \geq 0$).

---

### 4. Advanced Proofs

#### 4.1 Composition with Convex Functions
**Claim:** If $u$ is harmonic and $\phi$ is smooth and convex, then $v = \phi(u)$ is subharmonic.

**Proof:**
Using the chain rule for the Laplacian:
$$\Delta v = \nabla \cdot (\phi'(u) \nabla u) = \phi''(u)|\nabla u|^2 + \phi'(u) \Delta u$$
Since $u$ is harmonic ($\Delta u = 0$):
$$\Delta v = \phi''(u)|\nabla u|^2$$
Because $\phi$ is convex, $\phi'' \geq 0$, hence $\Delta v \geq 0$.

#### 4.2 The Subharmonicity of the Gradient Norm
**Claim:** If $u$ is harmonic, then $v = |Du|^2$ is subharmonic.

**Proof (The Hessian Approach):**
We calculate $\Delta v = \Delta \left( \sum_{i=1}^n u_{x_i}^2 \right)$:
$$\Delta v = \sum_{j=1}^n \frac{\partial^2}{\partial x_j^2} \left( \sum_{i=1}^n u_{x_i}^2 \right)$$
Following the expansion in the notes:
$$\Delta v = 2 \sum_{i,j=1}^n (u_{x_ix_j})^2 + 2 \sum_{i=1}^n u_{x_i} (\Delta u)_{x_i}$$
Since $u$ is harmonic, $\Delta u = 0$, and the second term vanishes. We are left with:
$$\Delta v = 2 |D^2 u|^2 \geq 0$$
where $|D^2 u| = \left( \sum_{i,j=1}^n u_{x_ix_j}^2 \right)^{1/2}$ is the **Frobenius norm of the Hessian matrix**. Since this is a sum of squares, it is strictly non-negative, proving $v$ is subharmonic.

### 5. The Strong Maximum Principle
If $u$ is harmonic in a connected region $U$:
1.  **Weak:** $\max_{\bar{U}} u = \max_{\partial U} u$.
2.  **Strong:** If there exists $x_0 \in U$ such that $u(x_0) = \max_{\bar{U}} u$, then $u$ is constant within $U$.

**Why? (Topological Insight):**
The set where $u(x) = M$ is both **open** (by the MVP) and **relatively closed** (by continuity). In a connected set, the only non-empty set that is both open and closed is the set itself. Thus, $u \equiv M$.
